Entities

The first step in design is concerned about choosing and documenting the columns in a table. The design phase does not care about how many rows are added, or about any value from an object.

Compact Representation

Entity-Relationship Diagram

An Entity-Relationship Diagram (ERD) is a convention for describing databases graphically. It is supported by many diagramming and design tools. There may be variations in exactly how they draw the design, but the basics of how entities and relations are drawn are pretty much universal. It is important to know this visual language to communicate with other designers.

Some tools:

  • diagrams.net (free, open source)
  • pen and paper (very effective for small designs; revisions and additions become harder to do as the design evolves)
  • Lucid Charts (free)
  • Visio (commercial)

We will cover the Relation features of ERDs when we introduce the concept of relations later.

ERD Entity

Entities are descriptions of tables. Lets say were making a database; we are going to put people into the database, and we need a contact email for them. We are going to call our table person, and when populated, it is going to end up looking like this:

 id |     name     |            email            
----+--------------+-----------------------------
  1 | Hamid Brown  | hamidb@outlook.com
  2 | Ha Li        | hali@gmail.com

The smallest step in design is to start with we are going to put people in our database. A generic example of one of those will be an entity called person.

ERD Entity Attributes

For each row representing a person that were going to put in our person table, there is certain information we need to know. Our requirements are that we have a contact email. And we probably want to know the persons name.

Each category of information we want to attach to our person is called an attribute. Each attribute adds to our knowledge about an entity.

Again working backwards from the table, what we will want for every person is:

person: id |  name  |  email

(This is just the column headers from the table.)

Attribute annotations

As the design gets more detailed and starts to approach the physical layout of the database, attributes may be annotated to indicate:

  • what kind of datatype they should be
  • if they are a key or part of a key (keys are next)
  • if they help implement a relation (relations are later)

Choosing Attributes

Attributes should differentiate.

The Attributes are usually not build instructions or a has-a list. Every customer is carbon-based, that is not an attribute a database needs to store separately for every person.

When looking at attributes, you are looking for things you need to differentiate, or to track. What is special about the entity?

Summary

  • Design is about the shape of tables, not any individual row
  • the ERD (Entity-Relationship Diagram) is a tool database developers use
  • an Entity in design represents any row in a table
  • an Attribute in design represents a cloumn in a table

Things to remember about entities:

  • they are abstract
  • they are part of the design
  • they are variables; placeholders

Data gets stored in tables, and tables get built after design is done.
